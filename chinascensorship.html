Introduction

For too long, the Chinese government has controlled both traditional media and new media to prevent the undermining of their authority. Their tactics for strict media control include:

  • Monitoring systems and firewalls
  • Shuttering publications or websites
  • Jailing dissident journalists, bloggers, and activists

Google and the Chinese government have recently clashed over internet censorship and the Norwegian Nobel Committee's awarding the 2010 Peace Prize to jailed Chinese activist Liu Xiaobo. This incident has since increased international awareness of China's censorship issues (Xu and Albert). You can learn more about their battle here.

How Free Is China's Media?

China’s constitution does afford its citizens freedom of speech and press, however due to the opacity of Chinese media regulations, authorities often take down news stories by claiming that they expose state secrets and endanger the country. Popular websites such as Wikipedia, Facebook, Twitter, YouTube, and some Google services are either fully blocked or temporarily “blacked out” during periods of controversy. China was ranked last by Freedom House for the second consecutive year out of sixty-five countries that represent 88 percent of the world's internet users (Xu and Albert).

Censorship Groups in China

Over a dozen government bodies review and enforce laws related to information flow within, into, and out of China. The most powerful censorship group in China is the Communist Party’s Central Propaganda Department (CPD). The CDP coordinates with General Administration of Press and Publication and State Administration of Radio, Film, and Television to ensure content promotes the party's beliefs (Xu and Albert). You can learn more about CPD here.

The Chinese government also employs large sums of people to monitor and censor China's media. According to a 2013 report by Beijing News, 2 million workers are responsible for reviewing internet posts using keyword searches and generating reports for "decision makers" (Xu and Albert).

Opposing Censorship

Despite heavy systematic control of the media, China's general public have found ways to combat the censorship. These methods include:

  • Setting up proxy servers and VPNs to avoid controls
  • Inserting new IP addresses into host files
  • Using SSH tunnels to route all internet traffic through a remote server
